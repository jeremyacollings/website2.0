Dating back to Darwin, we have held an intuitive belief that differences between competing species are key to understanding whether competition leads to exclusion or coexistence. Advances in ecological theory attributed to Chesson’s Coexistence Theory affirmed that species differences may promote coexistence by increasing species’ self-limitation (increasing niche differences) or may promote exclusion by exacerbating innate fitness asymmetries between species (increasing fitness inequalities). A class of species differences that have received great attention in recent decades has been the species-specific associations between macro-organisms and microorganisms. To date, very little research has been done on how divergence of species-specific microbial communities scales up to influence competitive dynamics of host organisms. 

During my Ph.D. at the University of Oregon, I conducted a common garden competition experiment with eight species of annual plants native to Willamette Valley prairies. By collecting fitness data from focal plants along competition gradients, I was able to fit annual plant population models and estimate intrinsic growth rates and competition coefficients for all species. Concurrently, I sequenced soil fungal DNA from the experiment to characterize plant species-specific fungal communities (the mycobiome) and estimated average dissimilarity between plant species mycobiomes. I paired these molecular fungal data with the plant demographic data to evaluate how mycobiome dissimilarity was related to niche & fitness differences, and what the consequence of these associations was for species coexistence. 

You can read more about this study in the associated publication in the Journal of Ecology.
